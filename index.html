Author's Abstract

I have spent from my first Spanish course in the second grade to now studying Spanish and the cultures that it embodies and it has become a passion of mine. Over the years that passion has grown to encompass language as a discipline instead of one particular language. Originally I had planned on this project being a photo journal from my, at the time, upcoming study abroad trip to Barcelona. Taking pictures in local cultural locations, historical sites, and other citywide locations. However, I had no idea how much that region would impact my perception on the world around me.

I spent roughly two months living in the heart of this Catalan city, studying its people, taking part in its traditions, learning about its history, and admiring its language. I even got to observe their modern secession movement first hand and spent time talking to locals and learning about their world. That is when this project was born. I was so enamored by this culture I had been a part of and observed as an outsider that I wanted to delve deeper into its roots and take the time to see what makes this culture so powerful. I was amazed by the people, and their obvious use of their own dialect over their national language and how that could be the symbol of their revolutionary spirit, which is where the idea of the "Dialect Effect" was born.

That is where this got interesting, because where Catalunya is trying to go is where many few revolutionary states ever truly achieve: secession. This is what truly inspired me to further my studies into this region. To look at what parts of their culture, language, history, and modern political landscape, in comparison to some of their neighboring Spanish states, have made this movement a near reality. Why does every citizen of Catalunya speak their regional dialect over Spanish even outside of major urban settings? Why is this Spanish state so primed for independence that the majority of its citizens are ready to break ties with its parent country? Finally, and most importantly, can this cultural, historical, and linguistic unity influence their modern political landscape in such a way that this may become their reality?
