The Rise of Catalunya

Post-Franco Catalunya

In 1969 Franco had designated price Juan Carlos de Borbon as the new Prince of Spain, effectively becoming his heir. In 1975 Franco slipped into a coma, and later that year was taken off of life support by his family giving Juan Carlos control of Spain.

Immediately, to the surprise of many in Spain, King Juan Carlos, the first monarch since 1931, introduced reforms that dismantled the Francoist regime and began the slow process into democracy in Spain. This included a Spanish constitution that was ratified in 1977. For Catalunya this meant the restoration of their Regional government the Generalitat under regional leadership instead of under national control. What this constitution also recognized was the existence of distinct national communities within Spain as a whole which started the re-regionalization movement in Spain.

Finally after years of fighting for their autonomy and being given either pseudo-autonomy or oppression, a statute in 1979 lead to full Catalan Autonomy. This statute also recognized Catalan as a separate “nationality” and the Catalan language was legalized and became the joint official language of the region. All of what Catalan people had fought for centuries to receive came through at the hands of the man who was expected to continue authoritarian rule in Franco’s stead.

Fifteen years later, after three regional presidents, Jodi Pujol, Pasqual Maragall, and Jos Montilla, and an Olympic games in 1992, there was a reform to the Catalan autonomy statute. This reform gave the regional government greater power and fiscal autonomy. It also gave official permission for Catalunya to describe themselves officially as a nation.-
